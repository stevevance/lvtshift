Chicago: who pays more, who pays less under a land value tax

A revenue-neutral model of shifting the City of Chicago's own property-tax levy (tax year 2024, $1.64 billion) from total property value toward land, across ~830,000 parcels of Cook County Assessor data. Built end-to-end with the LVTShift “/lvt-city” pipeline described in Progress & Poverty: “You can now vibe-code a land value tax.”

The whole county

Extending the model to all ~1.77M parcels of Cook County (every taxing body's full property tax, tax year 2024). The defensible versions are per taxing body — each of ~900 school/library/park/municipal/county districts shifts its own levy to land within its own boundary, revenue-neutral per district, so every district keeps its funding.

What the model finds

  • Vacant land and most housing pay more; building-heavy commercial pays less. Under the 2.5:1 split-rate, single-family homes rise ~29% (median), vacant land +135%, while offices and hotels fall ~54–55%.
  • Downtown commercial gets the biggest tax cuts. Only 10 of 77 community areas see their total bill fall — led by the Loop (−$90M), Near North (−$45M) and Near West (−$39M), where high-value offices and hotels pay much less — while the residential neighborhoods see small increases.
  • It's geographically progressive in dollars. High-land-value North Side neighborhoods (Lake View, Lincoln Park) bear far more in aggregate than the disinvested South Side, though vacant lots everywhere — concentrated on the South Side — see the largest increases.
Important caveat. The reform is modeled on market value, so it does two things at once: it shifts tax toward land and it unwinds Cook County's classification system (residential assessed at 10% of market vs. commercial at 25%). Much of the housing increase is the classification change, not the land shift — the classification-preserved variant isolates the two. The model also covers only the City's own levy (about 1.5 of a ~6.6 percentage-point composite bill) and runs on gross equalized value (homeowner/senior exemptions not applied).
