Who pays more, who pays less under a land value tax

Revenue-neutral models of shifting the property tax from total value toward land, built from Cook County Assessor and Clerk data for tax year 2024. Two jurisdictions are covered: the City of Chicago's own levy (~830,000 parcels), and the entire Cook County property tax — every taxing body combined, ~1.77 million parcels. Built end-to-end with the LVTShift pipeline described in Progress & Poverty: “You can now vibe-code a land value tax.”

How a land value tax shift works

Today your property tax is based on your whole property — the land plus whatever is built on it. Build an apartment, renovate, or add a story and your assessed value rises, so your tax bill rises. In effect, the current system charges you more for improving your property.

A land value tax shift moves the tax off the building and onto the land underneath it. The government collects the exact same total as before — it is revenue-neutral — but it gets there by charging a high rate on land and a low or zero rate on buildings.

Because the total is fixed, the shift is really a redistribution: your bill rises or falls depending on how much of your property's value is land versus building. A vacant lot or surface parking lot is almost all land, so it pays a lot more. A tall, dense building is mostly building on a small footprint, so it pays less. A typical single-family home lands somewhere in between. The reports below show two versions — a 2.5:1 split-rate (land taxed 2.5 times the building rate, the most Illinois law allows) and a 100% building abatement (buildings taxed nothing, a pure land tax). The goal is to stop penalizing people for building and instead tax land value, which owners did not create — nudging owners to develop and discouraging holding land idle.

What the Chicago model finds

  • Vacant land and most housing pay more; building-heavy commercial pays less. Under the 2.5:1 split-rate, single-family homes rise ~29% (median), vacant land +135%, while offices and hotels fall ~54–55%.
  • Downtown commercial gets the biggest tax cuts. Only 10 of 77 community areas see their total bill fall — led by the Loop (−$90M), Near North (−$45M) and Near West (−$39M), where high-value offices and hotels pay much less — while the residential neighborhoods see small increases.
  • It's geographically progressive in dollars. High-land-value North Side neighborhoods (Lake View, Lincoln Park) bear far more in aggregate than the disinvested South Side, though vacant lots everywhere — concentrated on the South Side — see the largest increases.
Important caveat. The reform is modeled on market value, so it does two things at once: it shifts tax toward land and it unwinds Cook County's classification system (residential assessed at 10% of market vs. commercial at 25%). Much of the housing increase is the classification change, not the land shift — the classification-preserved variant isolates the two. The model also covers only the City's own levy (about 1.5 of a ~6.6 percentage-point composite bill) and runs on gross equalized value (homeowner/senior exemptions not applied). A smaller idea: tax vacant land more, without a land value tax

A land value tax is a large structural change. A much smaller idea already fits inside Cook County's existing system, and it is not a land value tax — it leaves the whole tax structure alone and changes one number.

Cook County assesses vacant land at 10% of its market value — the same low rate as a house — while commercial property is assessed at 25%. So holding an empty lot is cheap, even on a busy commercial block. What if a vacant lot were assessed at 20% instead, partway toward the commercial rate? Moving a lot from 10% to 20% doubles its taxable value, so it pays roughly twice as much.

There are two ways to do this. In the revenue-neutral version, every taxing district keeps its exact budget: when vacant land's value goes up the rate ticks down a little, so a typical vacant lot pays about double while everyone else's bill drops by roughly 0.6%. In the other version, today's rates are left unchanged, so only vacant land pays more and the county simply collects about $125 million more. Either way, the point is to make sitting on an empty lot more expensive — a small, targeted nudge to build on it — without rebuilding the whole property tax.
